When 500 Computers Go Missing: Lessons from a Major Hotel Opening

I recently had the opportunity to join the pre-opening team for a major hotel opening (1500+ rooms!). That's one of the things I enjoy a great deal about being an IT manager for a global organization â€” although I work regionally, I am occasionally called upon to assist on task forces.

How Hotels Order Computers (Spoiler: It's Messy)

I worked on the computer inventory project, which sounds easy but is not. You'd think there would be a better way to order computers for a hotel, but it seems to consistently be a mess. Here's how the process works: each computer is tied to a job title that the hotel has received approval to hire, either now or in the future. Once the hotel leadership has the list of job titles, they sit down with the IT opening team. Together, the group decides for each job title whether they should receive a laptop, desktop, or no computer. Laptops (plus a monitor and dock) tend to be more expensive, so they are generally reserved for positions with more seniority. This means one must be careful when distributing equipment â€” if you give the assistant manager of housekeeping a laptop and tell the director of food and beverage, "Sorry! Can I give you a desktop instead?", they tend to be unhappy (and less productive, which is the real kicker.)

So, anyhow, the leadership team and IT team decide on which devices should be ordered and then they start ordering devices. One of the first complications of this process is that there is not one big order where a pallet with two tons of computers shows up at your newly-built hotel.

Operational work starts well before the hotel itself is ready for its staff to move in. You'll have a pre-opening office located near the hotel, and the team that is working at that office needs laptops and printers and other equipment to work. So, you'll do a smaller order or handful of orders to get the team initially up and running. Then you hire and hire and hire and all of a sudden you have directors and senior managers and you need more devices. So, you keep putting in orders and then at some point you just do the big push of submitting all of the remaining devices.

How I Approached the Inventory Crisis

By the time I arrived on site for the opening, the team had moved from the pre-opening office to the hotel itself. Plus, they'd been busy â€” about half of the 500 or so computers and laptops had already been distributed around the property. The other half were sitting in the conference room our team had taken over and filled with provisions from Costco (our war room, as we affectionately called it). Still, all this work had happened, and there was no complete inventory of where each device was located and where the remaining devices needed to be deployed.

This is a real business problem. As an IT leader, the last thing you want to be asking yourself (two weeks before opening) is "have we ordered enough computers? Do we need to order more?"

Or, even worse, "Did we accidentally give someone a computer that was not earmarked for them?"

Plus, inventories are just helpful for day to day work. You are miles ahead when preparing for capital expenditures when you know exactly when each device was purchased and when it is no longer covered by the manufacturer's support. You are able to troubleshoot faster and more effectively when you know what is where.

Before I arrived, four other people (including the hotel's director of technology) had attempted to build an inventory. There was no document that was iterated to create an up-to-date inventory â€” only a few snapshots from moments in time that recorded some (maybe 20%?) of the devices.

The IT project manager actually barked at me to not "waste my time building an inventory" and told me that building an inventory this late in the game was "impossible." In fact, she was so against the idea of the pre-opening team working on inventory that she refused to share the order sheet with anyone, including me. So, I had to be creative.

We have internal systems that I can use to see which devices are connected to the network. By looking across a few of them, I had a pretty good idea of how many devices had been physically set up on property. I knew this late in the game there would be no more devices shipped, so everything that was online now (either in its actual location or sitting in our conference room) was everything that we had. With that, I had a list of all of the devices and their serial numbers. I just then needed to find every computer â€” how hard could that be?

I started by just going around the hotel, and everywhere I could find a computer, I recorded the serial number of the device and its location. Within a few hours, I had identified about 80% of the devices.

By the end of the day, when the project manager saw how much progress I had made, she gave me kudos and said, "huh. Maybe you actually can solve this problem." And so I became one of the select few who had access to the order sheet.

With this information, I had more context to track down the remaining missing computers (who knew that hotels have harbormasters now? Or that they need computers?!).

With the order sheet, I was able to correlate deployed computers with the job titles those computers were ordered for, based on the physical location they were in. Even if you don't know (or don't care) which device was deployed to which of the twenty accountants, you can feel confident inventorying the twenty computers in the accounting office and marking those computers as deployed. Within another day, I had created a list, organized by department, showing how many computers had been deployed and how many needed to be deployed still (from the conference room computer storage). Luckily for me, there were no over-deployments (people who received computers despite one not being ordered for them). In fact, there was kind of the opposite problem â€” leftover (spare!!!) computers.

Having some spare computers isn't a terrible thing. It helps you respond to issues promptly. If a computer dies catastrophically somehow, you can simply warranty the dead device and replace it immediately with a spare. Still, you don't want to spend too much money proactively and then have devices you never use sitting around. A general rule for spares is 3â€“5% gives you a good balance to replace equipment quickly but not have wasted capital.

Key takeaways

Looking back, what started as a "waste of time" became one of the most valuable contributions I made to that opening. It reinforced my belief that sometimes the most important work happens in the unglamorous details that everyone else wants to skip.

That said, in your projects, document everything as you go. The reason this became such a mess in the first place was that no one maintained a living inventory document. Someone needs to either own the process from the very beginning or handoffs must ensure all relevant information is passed between parties appropriately. Every handoff between teams in this opening created information loss.
